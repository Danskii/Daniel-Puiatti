Starting a brand from scratch

Starting a brand from scratch takes grit, persistance and flexibility. Starting a brand from scratch is no joke.

At some point in December 2017 I decided I would try and sell products online. Real original, I know.

I think the idea took hold when I came across a Shopify advertisement promoting something called drop shipping as a way to generate income through ecommerce. The concept was seductively simple. Start up a trial ecommerce store with Shopify, connect your ecommerce store to an additional product called Oberlo, run some ads to drive traffic to your store and watch the money roll in.

Well, some money rolled in, but it was far less than was was rolling out. I had no success with Shopify or Oberlo. However, the notion that I could make this ecommerce thing work persisted in my mind. I felt determined.

Armed with the bitter sweet knowledge which falure so harshly imparts on memory, I started to consider the alternatives. And alternatives there were: baby products, pet food, trading cards, you name it. Almost every niche product has a market, and a blog trying to sell you on selling it.

Tshirts called out to me. I thought, OK I have some basic design skills, perhaps I can apply them. So I did.

Inspired by my failed cat themed ecommerce Shopify store I decided I would take the name and logo from the store and sell tshirts with it. I imagined hundreds of variants of the logo: city themes, sports themes, pirate themes, and on and on. But I started with one.

I spent some time applying my web development skills towards building out a website for the brand. Eventually I came up with something simple. I connected this brand page to a tshirt printing and fulfillment website called Teespring and on the seventh day I rested.

Alright, so I had the brand, the product and the website all ready to go, fancy tracking codes installed with analytics and A/B testing technology and newsletter signup form. Lovely I know. But I needed customers.

Customers do not manifest from the ether

This is where a stark realization took hold. All the time and effort I invested in building the aformationed product and website were meaningless unless I had customers.

Now, marketing. Now, advertising

Thus I began building customers. I decided I would, once a week, produce enough visual content featuring my brand so that I could publish a post on social daily.

Every Monday I sit down for an hour, source images, photoshop my logo onto them, schedule and publish content. I have been doing this for nearly two months now. Traffic is increasing minimally.

I decided I would suppliment this organic content with an advertising campaign. SO I build a funnel. I built some ads, I targeted some audiences and I let the campaign run. I optimized, refined and optimzied more, tested new creative, new copy. I let the campaign run.

Every day, slowly, more impressions, more awareness. Every week I spend another few hours creating organic content.

So far, one tshirt. That's all I sold. No one said building a brand from scratch would be easy.
